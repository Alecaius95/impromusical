A Field Guide for Improvisers

Music for the Stage
in the Moment

Instant genre signals · chord progressions · reference songs · emotional cues · improv principles

Each genre has 2–3 unmistakable signals you can deploy in the first two seconds. Progressions use Roman numerals (key-neutral). Y = YouTube search · S = Spotify search · 🎤 = primarily vocal song.

🤠

Western / Country

Saloon · Ranch · Frontier
Boom-chuck bassGuitar, Fiddle, Steel guitar🎹 A, D, G major
  • ▸ Root on beat 1, chord on beats 2&3 (waltz) or alternating (4/4)
  • ▸ Major pentatonic — open 5ths feel wide and dusty
  • ▸ Stiff, slightly square articulation; no rubato
  • ▸ Bright keys: A, D, G major
Avoid

Minor keys, legato phrasing, jazz chords, rubato — they all drain the dusty frontier feel instantly.

Chord Progressions
I – IV – V – II – V – IV – V
Keep changes slow (1–2 bars each). Add IV–I "amen" cadence at phrase ends.
Reference Songs
Hank Williams — Your Cheatin' Heart 🎤YS
Morricone — The Good, The Bad and The UglyYS
Dolly Parton — Jolene 🎤YS

Film Noir / Spy

Detective · Interrogation · 1950s intrigue
Sparse minor jazz chordsMuted trumpet, Alto sax, Bass🎹 D min · F min · Dorian
  • ▸ Half-diminished (ø) and minor 7 chords — leave silence
  • ▸ Walking bass feel, slow and suspicious
  • ▸ Tritone for danger — resolves nowhere comfortable
  • ▸ The silence IS the tension
Avoid

Busy passages, major keys, resolving cadences, or upbeat tempos — they break the cold, suspicious atmosphere.

Chord Progressions
im7 – iiø7 – V7 – imim – bVII – bVI – V7
Play each chord once per bar, staccato. Let the last chord hang in silence.
Reference Songs
Henry Mancini — The Pink Panther ThemeYS
Chet Baker — Almost Blue 🎤YS
John Barry — James Bond ThemeYS

Circus / Vaudeville

Clowns · Spectacle · Absurdity
Oom-pah bassTuba, Calliope, Brass band🎹 C, G, F major
  • ▸ Oom-pah: low root on 1, mid-chord on 2
  • ▸ Fast major key, staccato, bouncy
  • ▸ Chromatic runs up/down = pratfall
  • ▸ Sad clown: slow down, add minor seconds
Avoid

Legato lines, slow tempos, or complex harmonies — the circus lives and dies on relentless rhythmic momentum.

Chord Progressions
I – I7 – IV – ivI – VI – II – V
The I7 (dominant 7th on tonic) is the single most "circus" sound — use it immediately.

Epic / Historical Drama

Battle · Royal court · Ancient world
Stately block chordsStrings, Brass, Timpani, Organ🎹 D min · A min · Dorian
  • ▸ Slow, deliberate block chords — every note counts
  • ▸ Octaves in bass, 5ths in right hand = power
  • ▸ Modulate up a half step at the climactic moment
  • ▸ Dorian or Phrygian for ancient/mysterious settings
Avoid

Fast passages, light textures, or high registers as your base — epic lives in the low-mid range, loud and weighty.

Chord Progressions
i – VII – VI – VIII – V – vi – III – IV – I – IV – V
The IV–V ending feels triumphant and inevitable. Minor with raised VII = Dorian, ancient and noble.
Reference Songs
Hans Zimmer — Now We Are Free (Gladiator)YS
Carl Orff — O Fortuna 🎤YS
John Williams — Imperial MarchYS

Gothic / Horror

Haunted house · Villain entrance · Dread
Diminished chordsPipe organ, Strings, Theremin🎹 D min · Phrygian
  • ▸ Diminished 7th chord = instant creepy (stack minor 3rds)
  • ▸ Tremolos in low register for sustained dread
  • ▸ Chromatic descending bassline (Lamento bass)
  • ▸ Single high note after silence = jump scare cue
Avoid

Major keys, bright registers, staccato bounce, or anything melodically resolved — horror needs to stay unresolved and heavy.

Chord Progressions
i – viiø – i – V7i – bVII – bVI – V (Lamento)
The chromatic descent i–VII–VI–V is the oldest "dread" pattern in Western music.
Reference Songs
Bach — Toccata & Fugue in D minorYS
Nick Cave — Red Right Hand 🎤YS

Pastoral / Nature

Garden · Countryside · Fairy tale forest
Flowing arpeggiosFlute, Harp, Acoustic guitar, Oboe🎹 G, D, F major · Lydian
  • ▸ Rolling 6/8 or 12/8 arpeggios, wide voicings
  • ▸ Major with sus2/sus4 for airiness
  • ▸ Pentatonic melodies never clash
  • ▸ Whole-tone scale for magical/dream quality
Avoid

Staccato, percussive attacks, minor keys, or dense low-register chords — the pastoral world needs air and light.

Chord Progressions
I – V/vi – vi – IVIsus2 – V – vi – IVadd9
Suspensions (add9, sus2) soften every chord. Try a 5th pedal tone in the bass throughout.
Reference Songs
Debussy — Clair de LuneYS
Enya — May It Be 🎤YS
Howard Shore — The Shire (LOTR)YS

Sci-Fi / Space

Spaceship · Alien encounter · Future
Quartal / atonal chordsSynth pads, Theremin, Strings🎹 No home key · sus2 · sus4
  • ▸ Quartal harmony (stack 4ths) = instantly "space"
  • ▸ Held sus2/sus4 chords with no resolution
  • ▸ Sparse, slow — space is vast and silent
  • ▸ Dissonant minor 2nds in high register for danger
Avoid

Resolving V7–I cadences, waltz bass patterns, or anything that sounds "of the earth" — resolution feels too human for space.

Chord Progressions
Isus2 – bVII – bVI – bVIIIm(maj7) – bVII – bVI – V
Avoid resolving V7→I — it feels too human. Let chords drift without landing anywhere.
Reference Songs
John Williams — Star Wars Main ThemeYS
David Bowie — Space Oddity 🎤YS
Vangelis — Blade Runner BluesYS

Ragtime / Jazz Age

1920s party · Speakeasy · Comedy chase
Syncopated right handUpright piano, Trombone, Clarinet🎹 F, C, Bb major
  • ▸ Steady LH (octave–chord), syncopated RH
  • ▸ Bright tempo — dragging kills the feel
  • ▸ Secondary dominants: VI7, II7 in major keys
  • ▸ Glissando = punchline or chase moment
Avoid

Slow tempos, legato playing, or minor keys — dragging the tempo even slightly collapses the ragtime feel entirely.

Chord Progressions
I – I7 – IV – iv – I – V7 – II – VI7 – II7 – V7 – I
Secondary dominants (VI7, II7) are the ragtime signature — every chord pulls toward the next.
Reference Songs
Scott Joplin — Maple Leaf RagYS

Romantic / Melodrama

Love scene · Reunion · Emotional peak
Sweeping melody, rubatoStrings, Piano, Cello🎹 Db, Ab, Eb major
  • ▸ Rubato — bend tempo to the emotional weight
  • ▸ Rich 9th and 7th chords for depth
  • ▸ Ascend melodically as intensity builds
  • ▸ iv → I (minor 4 to major tonic) = tearjerker
Avoid

Staccato, steady metronomic tempo, or thin single-note lines — romance needs richness, sustain, and rhythmic flexibility.

Chord Progressions
I – V – vi – IVvi – IV – I – V
The vi–IV–I–V variant starts on the sad side and lifts. Add iv (minor 4) before any final I for maximum heartache.
Reference Songs
Morricone — Cinema Paradiso ThemeYS
Chopin — Nocturne Op. 9 No. 2YS
Celine Dion — My Heart Will Go On 🎤YS

Latin / Flamenco

Tango · Fiesta · Passion · Confrontation
Phrygian / AndalusianClassical guitar, Accordion, Castanets🎹 A min · E Phrygian dominant
  • ▸ Flamenco: Phrygian dominant (1–b2–3–4–5–b6–b7) over E or A
  • ▸ Tango: sharp staccato chords, strong downbeat, dramatic pauses
  • ▸ Bossa nova: maj7 and 9 chords, gentle syncopation
  • ▸ Sudden stop then restart = instant flair
Avoid

Soft dynamics, plain major tonality, or metronomic evenness — Latin styles live in rhythmic sharpness and passionate extremes.

Chord Progressions
i – bVII – bVI – V (Andalusian)im – ivm – V7 – im
The Andalusian cadence IS flamenco. Tango hammers the minor i–V7 tension harder and more staccato.
Reference Songs

Musical Theatre

Big number · Soliloquy · Showstopper
Full Broadway vampShow piano, Strings, Brass🎹 C, F, Bb, G major
  • ▸ "Vamp": repeat a 2–4 bar intro to set key and lock performers in
  • ▸ Strong downbeats, very clear harmonic rhythm (1–2 chords per bar)
  • ▸ Modulate up a half/whole step for the final chorus — always
  • ▸ Last chord: full register, fortissimo, held. Button everything.
Avoid

Tentative or quiet endings, vague harmonic rhythm, or skipping the final key modulation — musicals demand commitment and declaration.

Chord Progressions
I – IV – V – II – vi – ii – V7 – I
The "I–vi–ii–V" circle progression is the Broadway workhorse. Add chromatic passing chords between any two for flavor.
Reference Songs
Sondheim — Being Alive (Company) 🎤YS
Lloyd Webber — Memory (Cats) 🎤YS
Kander & Ebb — All That Jazz (Chicago) 🎤YS

Cinematic / Film Score

Slow motion · Montage · Big reveal
Slow harmonic rhythmOrchestra, Piano, Choir, Synth🎹 A min · D min · any key
  • ▸ Very long chords — sometimes 4–8 bars on one harmony
  • ▸ Ostinato in one hand, melody in the other
  • ▸ Dynamics do the narrative work: pp → fff over 16 bars
  • ▸ "Stinger": loud, sudden, dissonant chord on a reveal moment
Avoid

Fast harmonic rhythm or busy melodic runs — cinematic scoring is about space, swell, and the single perfectly-timed moment.

Chord Progressions
i – bVI – bIII – bVIII – bVII – IV – I
The minor "i–bVI–bIII–bVII" is in nearly every modern blockbuster. Major "I–bVII–IV" sounds heroic yet uncertain — perfect for bittersweet moments.
Reference Songs
Hans Zimmer — Time (Inception)YS
Adele — Skyfall 🎤YS
Max Richter — On the Nature of DaylightYS

Soap Opera / Telenovela

Revelation · Betrayal · Dramatic stare
Melodrama + stingersStrings, Piano, Organ stab🎹 F min · D min · any minor
  • ▸ Sudden stinger chord on any revelation or slap
  • ▸ Slow, over-emotive string underscore on everything
  • ▸ "Dun dun DUN" = three ascending notes, last one held. Always works.
  • ▸ Hard stop → silence → resume louder = the freeze moment
Avoid

Subtlety, understatement, or quiet resolutions — soap opera emotion is always turned to 11. If in doubt, make it more dramatic.

Chord Progressions
i – V7 – i (stinger)IV – V – vi (deceptive cadence)
The deceptive cadence (IV→V→vi) is the soap opera pivot: you expect I, but you land on vi (minor). Total shock — every time.
Reference Songs
Barry DeVorzon — Nadia's Theme (Y&R)YS
Whitney Houston — I Will Always Love You 🎤YS
Various — Dramatic soap opera stingerYS

Sexy / Sensual

Seduction · Slow burn · Tension
Slow minor grooveSaxophone, Muted trumpet, Electric piano🎹 A min · D min · Dorian
  • ▸ Slow tempo, syncopated — always slightly behind the beat
  • ▸ Minor 7 and 9 chords: lush, warm, unresolved
  • ▸ Bass note first, chord follows after a lazy half-beat
  • ▸ Stay on one chord far longer than expected — repetition IS seduction
Avoid

Bright major keys, staccato, fast tempos, or on-the-beat playing — anything crisp or energetic breaks the languid tension immediately.

Chord Progressions
im9 – IV9 – im9 – V7susi7 – bVII9 – bVI(maj7) – V7
The V7sus4 wants to resolve but doesn't. Stay on it uncomfortably long. That IS the feeling.
Reference Songs
Marvin Gaye — Let's Get It OnYS
Sade — No Ordinary LoveYS
Barry White — Can't Get Enough of Your LoveYS

Religious / Choir

Church · Funeral · Ceremony · Revelation
Hymn-style block chordsPipe organ, Choir, Strings, Bells🎹 C, F, G, Bb major
  • ▸ Four-part hymn voicing: soprano melody on top, bass below, inner voices move minimally
  • ▸ Slow, steady tempo — no syncopation, no rubato; every beat is equal
  • ▸ "Amen" cadence (IV–I) as a phrase ending signals sacred space instantly
  • ▸ For gospel: add a swung feel, blue notes, and a call-and-response pattern in the right hand
  • ▸ Plagal cadence (IV→I) feels devotional; perfect cadence (V→I) feels more triumphant/conclusive
Avoid

Syncopation, jazzy voicings, or flashy runs in a sacred context — reverence comes from restraint. Save gospel energy for when the scene explicitly calls for it.

Chord Progressions
I – IV – I – V – I (hymn)I – vi – IV – V – I
The plagal "Amen" (IV–I) is the single most recognizable sacred cue. For gospel, try I–I7–IV–iv–I with a swung 8th-note feel.
Reference Songs
Handel — Hallelujah Chorus (Messiah)YS
Bach — Jesu, Joy of Man's DesiringYS
Mahalia Jackson — How Great Thou ArtYS

When no genre is set, these emotional "colors" are your go-to palette. Each feeling has a core technique, chord progressions, and reference pieces. 🎤 = primarily vocal song.

😂 Comic / Silly

Bright major, staccato, slightly rushed. One wrong high note at the end = the joke.
🎹 C, G, D majorUpright piano, Xylophone, Tuba
Core Trick Play something perfectly normal, then add one jarring note. Mismatch IS the punchline. Progressions I – I7 – IV – VI – bVII – IV – I Listen To Yakety Sax (Benny Hill theme) · Scott Joplin: The Entertainer · Weird Al Yankovic: Any Song 🎤 Avoid Minor keys, legato, slow tempos, or anything that lingers emotionally.

😢 Sad / Tender

Minor key, slow, legato. Dorian = wistful; natural minor = tragic.
🎹 D min · A min · DorianStrings, Cello, Soft piano
Core Trick Play melody with rubato — let certain notes linger. Silence between phrases = grief. Progressions im – iv – bVII – IIIim – bVI – bIII – V Listen To Barber: Adagio for Strings · Radiohead: Exit Music 🎤 · Arvo Pärt: Spiegel im Spiegel Avoid Staccato, bright major keys, steady metronomic tempo, or busy right-hand activity.

😨 Tense / Suspense

Repeat a low note, slowly increase density. The tritone is your best friend.
🎹 Phrygian · diminished · chromaticLow strings, Bass piano, Organ
Core Trick Ostinato: one repeated low figure that never resolves. The brain waits — and doesn't get it. Progressions i – viiø – i – viiøi – bII – i – bII Listen To Bernard Herrmann: Psycho strings · Jaws theme · Nine Inch Nails: Something I Can Never Have 🎤 Avoid Resolving cadences (V→I), major tonality, or anything that feels like it "lands" — resolution kills tension dead.

😍 Romance / Wonder

Slow arpeggios, major, lush 7th and 9th chords. Let melody float above.
🎹 Db, Ab, Eb majorStrings, Harp, Flute, Soft piano
Core Trick Sustain a chord, add melody note an octave above — creates yearning open space. Progressions Imaj7 – V – vi – IVmaj7I – iii – IV – I Listen To Debussy: La Mer · Ravel: Pavane · Elvis Costello: She 🎤 Avoid Staccato, busy rhythms, sharp attacks, or anything in a stark high register.

⚡ Urgent / Action

Fast 16th notes, driving bass, no rests. Keep harmony simple — no time for complexity.
🎹 E min · B minBrass, Percussion, Full orchestra
Core Trick Repeated-note LH ostinato at speed. RH plays jabbing figures. Accelerate gradually. Progressions i – VII – i – VIIi – v – i – VI – VII Listen To Prokofiev: Montagues & Capulets · Queen: Don't Stop Me Now 🎤 · Mission Impossible theme Avoid Rubato, slow harmonic changes, soft dynamics, or major keys — they drain urgency instantly.

🧘 Calm / Contemplative

Very slow harmonic changes, wide voicings, open space. Let silence do the work.
🎹 G, C major · LydianFlute, Acoustic guitar, Vibraphone
Core Trick Single sustained chord in mid-register. Move just one note at a time, very slowly. Progressions Isus2 – Isus4 – I – VI – II – I – II (Lydian) Listen To Satie: Gymnopédie No. 1 · Nick Drake: Pink Moon 🎤 · Brian Eno: Music for Airports Avoid Fast notes, dense chords, dissonance, or dramatic dynamic swings — they shatter contemplative stillness.

😤 Anger / Confrontation

Heavy accents on downbeats, low register, minor key with tritones. Sudden dynamic spikes.
🎹 Phrygian · Locrian · chromaticLow piano, Brass stabs, Timpani
Core Trick Minor i to bII (Neapolitan) hammered repeatedly — aggressive and unstable. Progressions i – bII – i – bIIi – v – bVI – bVII – i Listen To Beethoven: 5th Symphony opening · Rage Against the Machine: Killing In the Name 🎤 · Stravinsky: Rite of Spring Avoid Soft dynamics, arpeggios, major tonality, or anything melodically gentle — it completely undercuts the confrontation.

🎉 Triumph / Joy

Loud major chords, strong downbeat, brass sound. Picardy third (major ending) = victory punch.
🎹 Eb, Bb, F majorBrass, Strings, Full orchestra
Core Trick IV → I loud major. Final chord rings out. Completely predictable — that's why it works. Progressions I – IV – V – IbVII – IV – I (rock ending) Listen To Beethoven: Ode to Joy · Queen: We Are the Champions 🎤 · John Williams: Raiders March Avoid Minor keys, unresolved chords, quiet dynamics, or trailing endings — triumph demands a loud, decisive landing.

Contemporary and popular song genres — useful when a scene calls for a specific musical world a real band might occupy. Progressions use Roman numerals. 🎤 = primarily vocal reference · Y = YouTube · S = Spotify.

Rock

Rebellion · Energy · Attitude · Angst
Power chords, strong backbeatElectric guitar, Bass, Drums🎹 E, A, D minor/major
  • ▸ Heavy beats on 2 and 4 — the backbeat IS rock
  • ▸ Power chord (root + 5th, no 3rd) = instant grit; ambiguous major/minor
  • ▸ bVII chord is the rock signature — outside the key, always sounds right
  • ▸ Pentatonic minor scale for riffs; add the b5 blue note for edge
Avoid

Soft legato lines, jazz voicings, or delicate arpeggios — rock lives on attack, grit, and rhythmic punch.

Chord Progressions
I – bVII – IV – Ii – bVII – bVI – bVII
The bVII is the rock "money chord" — borrowed from parallel minor, sounds powerful in any context.
Reference Songs
The Rolling Stones — Jumpin' Jack Flash 🎤YS
AC/DC — Highway to Hell 🎤YS
Nirvana — Come As You Are 🎤YS

Metal

Intensity · Darkness · Power · Chaos
Low riffs, tritones, speedDistorted guitar, Bass, Drums🎹 E min · Phrygian · diminished
  • ▸ Tritone (b5) interval is the "devil's interval" — pure metal darkness
  • ▸ Phrygian mode: i–bII hammered — the single most metal sound at a piano
  • ▸ Low register, heavy attack, almost no sustain on single notes
  • ▸ Sudden dynamic drops to nothing, then back to full force
Avoid

Anything polite, warm, or harmonically resolved — metal needs aggression, darkness, and relentless forward momentum.

Chord Progressions
i – bII – i – bIIi – bVI – bVII – i
Phrygian i–bII is instantly metal. The bVI–bVII–i resolution feels both inevitable and crushing.
Reference Songs
Black Sabbath — Iron Man 🎤YS
Metallica — Enter Sandman 🎤YS
Rammstein — Du Hast 🎤YS

Folk

Storytelling · Simplicity · Nature · Community
Open chords, fingerpickedAcoustic guitar, Banjo, Fiddle, Mandolin🎹 G, D, C major · Mixolydian
  • ▸ Simple diatonic chords — I, IV, V, and maybe vi. No fancy extensions
  • ▸ bVII chord (Mixolydian) gives an earthy, unresolved folk flavour
  • ▸ Fingerpicked arpeggios or alternating bass — never strummed block chords
  • ▸ Leave space — folk trusts silence and lets the lyric breathe
Avoid

Jazz extensions, complex rhythms, big dynamics, or dense textures — folk is intimate, honest, and stripped back.

Chord Progressions
I – V – vi – IVI – bVII – IV – I
I–V–vi–IV is the universal folk/pop backbone. The Mixolydian bVII version gives a more ancient, rootsy feel.
Reference Songs
Bob Dylan — Blowin' in the Wind 🎤YS
Simon & Garfunkel — The Sound of Silence 🎤YS

Jazz

Sophistication · Cool · Late nights · Improvisation
ii–V–I, extensions, substitutionsPiano, Double bass, Drums, Trumpet🎹 Bb, F, Eb major · Dorian
  • ▸ ii7–V7–I is the core jazz move — play it in multiple keys rapidly
  • ▸ Add 7ths, 9ths, 11ths, 13ths to every chord — plain triads sound wrong
  • ▸ Swing feel: triplet-based 8th notes, long-short-long-short pattern
  • ▸ Comping: sparse, syncopated left hand; don't lock to a beat
Avoid

Plain triads, metronomic evenness, on-the-beat comping, or resolving every phrase — jazz breathes in the spaces.

Chord Progressions
iim7 – V7 – Imaj7Imaj7 – vim7 – iim7 – V7
The ii–V–I is jazz DNA. The full turnaround (I–vi–ii–V) cycles endlessly — perfect for indefinite vamping.
Reference Songs
Miles Davis — Autumn LeavesYS
Ella Fitzgerald — Summertime 🎤YS
Dave Brubeck — Take FiveYS

Pop

Catchy · Upbeat · Relatable · Commercial
Four-chord loop, hooksPiano, Synth, Programmed drums, Bass🎹 C, G, A, D major
  • ▸ I–V–vi–IV is the universal pop loop — it literally works for hundreds of songs
  • ▸ Keep harmony simple and repetitive; the hook is everything
  • ▸ Strong four-on-the-floor pulse or clear backbeat — never ambiguous
  • ▸ Modulate up a half step for the final chorus — instant emotional lift
Avoid

Complex harmony, modal ambiguity, or long instrumental passages — pop is about the vocal hook above everything else.

Chord Progressions
I – V – vi – IVvi – IV – I – V
These two progressions use exactly the same four chords, just starting from a different point. Recognisable from the first bar.

Singer-Songwriter

Vulnerability · Introspection · Confessional · Intimate
Fingerstyle, open voicingsAcoustic piano, Acoustic guitar, Cello🎹 C, G, E major · relative minors
  • ▸ Sparse texture — one hand only is often enough; silence is intentional
  • ▸ Add 2nds and 4ths to chords (add9, sus2) for that open, unresolved feeling
  • ▸ Rubato is acceptable — follow the emotional arc, not the metronome
  • ▸ The melody is the voice; accompany beneath it, never compete with it
Avoid

Dense arrangements, steady metronomic pulse, or full block chords — the intimacy lives in restraint and space.

Chord Progressions
Iadd9 – V – vi – IVadd9I – iii – IV – I
The add9 versions of I and IV give warmth without jazz complexity. iii (mediant) chord adds harmonic colour without going chromatic.

Itpop / Cantautorato

Bittersweet · Poetic · Mediterranean · Romance
Lush chords, melodic bassPiano, Acoustic guitar, Strings, Synth pad🎹 C, F, G major · relative minors
  • ▸ Melancholy and beauty coexist — major key with minor vi or iv colours
  • ▸ Stepwise melodic bass lines under chords (like a cello countermelody)
  • ▸ Sustained piano chords with light arpeggiated guitar texture above
  • ▸ Chromatic passing chords and unexpected borrowed chords from parallel minor
Avoid

Hard rhythmic attacks, aggressive dynamics, or overly dense textures — itpop floats between sadness and warmth, never extreme.

Chord Progressions
I – vi – IV – VI – V – iv – I
The minor iv (borrowed from parallel minor) dropped into a major key gives that distinctly Italian bittersweet pang — think Battiato or De André.

Funk / Soul

Groove · Swagger · Dancefloor · Joy
Tight 16th-note grooveRhodes piano, Bass, Horns, Drums🎹 E, A, D minor · Dorian
  • ▸ The groove is everything — lock to a single chord for bars at a time
  • ▸ Dominant 9 chord (I9) is the funk tonic — never plain major or minor
  • ▸ Syncopate the bass line heavily: anticipate beats 2 and 4
  • ▸ Short, stabby chords on the off-beats (the "and" of beats)
Avoid

Long sustains, metronomic on-the-beat playing, or complex harmonic movement — funk is about rhythmic feel, not harmonic sophistication.

Chord Progressions
I9 – IV9 – I9 – V9im7 – IV9 – im7 – bVII9
A single dominant 9th chord vamped for 8+ bars IS the groove. Add IV9 only when you need a brief lift.
Reference Songs

Reggae / Ska

Laid-back · Caribbean · Protest · Sunshine
Offbeat skank, one-dropOrgan, Bass, Guitar, Drums🎹 A, D, G major/minor
  • ▸ The "skank": short chords on beats 2 and 4 only — nothing on 1 and 3
  • ▸ Bass is the lead voice — melodic and prominent; chords are just decoration
  • ▸ Ska is the faster upstroke version: all 4 offbeats, bright and choppy
  • ▸ Keep it simple — I, IV, V, and the occasional minor vi
Avoid

On-the-beat chord playing, complex harmony, or rushing the tempo — reggae derives all its tension from what's held back.

Chord Progressions
I – IV – V – IVi – VII – IV – V
Play each chord as a short offbeat stab. The bass carries the melodic motion; the piano/guitar simply marks the harmony.
Reference Songs
Bob Marley — No Woman, No Cry 🎤YS
The Specials — A Message to You Rudy 🎤YS
Jimmy Cliff — The Harder They Come 🎤YS

Blues

Suffering · Release · Grit · Late night
12-bar, dominant 7ths, blue notesPiano, Harmonica, Electric guitar, Bass🎹 E, A, G major · pentatonic minor
  • ▸ 12-bar form: I7 (4 bars) – IV7 (2) – I7 (2) – V7 – IV7 – I7 – V7
  • ▸ Every chord is a dominant 7th — even the I and IV, which would be "wrong" in classical
  • ▸ Blue notes: flatten the 3rd, 5th, and 7th for that bitter, bent sound
  • ▸ Shuffle feel: swing the 8th notes (long-short) at a medium walking tempo
Avoid

Improv demands instant pivots. These are your practical tools for shifting the music when the scene changes.

Genre / Mood Pivots

The Edit Signal

When a scene ends, land on a clear tonic chord and let it ring. Silence + pause = "scene over." Don't trail off ambiguously — make the ending deliberate.

The Pivot Chord

Some chords exist in multiple keys. C major to A minor: an E minor chord works in both — play it, then re-harmonize. Shift mood without a hard cut.

Texture Shift

Change what you're doing without changing key. Arpeggios → block chords, or legato → staccato signals the scene has changed gear. Useful when you don't know where they're going yet.

Half-Step Up

Modulating up a half step mid-phrase = instant escalation. Use it when you see the scene about to explode. It sounds exactly like the movie moment kicking in.

The Slow Burn

Gradually slow tempo and drop volume to signal an intimate or introspective turn. Audiences follow tempo closely — they'll physically quiet down with you.

Sudden Stop

A hard stop on a dissonant note (diminished chord) reads as: something has gone terribly wrong. Very useful for reversals or shock moments. Then silence.

Staying in the Scene

The Underscore Default

When unsure, play a long, soft, slow sustained chord in a neutral register. It says "something is happening" without committing to a genre. Buy yourself four bars to read the scene.

Follow the Body, Not the Words

Physicality usually leads dialogue in improv. When someone slouches and slows down, drop the music before they say a sad line. You'll seem prescient.

Mirror Volume to Stage Energy

Two people whispering? Drop to nearly nothing. A big group scene? Fill the room. Let the stage's dynamic range tell you how loud to be.

Leave Space for Laughs

When comedy lands, briefly stop or drop to just a hint of sound. Continuing over laughter muffles it and kills the moment. Resume as the wave subsides.

You already know improv's foundational rules. Here's how they translate directly into the musician's chair.

"You're not accompanying a scene. You're a scene partner who speaks only in music."

Yes, And — in music

If a performer establishes a Western scene and you're playing noir jazz, you're blocking them. Accept what they've set and add to it. Commit immediately to what the stage tells you.

Don't Comment — Support

Resist the urge to be clever. If someone says "I'm so happy," you don't need to play ironic minor. Play happy. The ensemble's coherence is the art form.

The Musician as Narrator

You can signal time jumps, location changes, and emotional states before they're spoken. Use this sparingly — a stinger or theme change is powerful precisely because it's rare.

Economy of Notes

The most common beginner mistake is playing too much. A single sustained bass note says more than a busy right-hand run. Let rests breathe. Space is musical punctuation.

Tempo Is Emotion

Before melody or harmony, tempo sends the signal. Fast = energy, comedy, action, anxiety. Slow = intimacy, sadness, gravity, dread. Get the tempo right first; everything else can be rough.

Strong Initiations

Choose something specific and commit in the first two seconds. A bold choice that's slightly wrong beats vague neutrality — the performers will adapt to you.

The Emotional Offer

Sometimes you're not reflecting the scene — you're offering a gift. A mysterious musical phrase as a performer enters a space gives them an emotion to play with. It's an "offer" in improv terms.

Reading the Room

Watch the audience, not just the performers. If laughs are slow to land, the tempo might need lifting. If everyone's leaning forward in silence, sustain that — don't break it with a music joke.

The Rule of Two Hands

Under stress you'll revert to what's automatic. Practice five specific patterns (waltz bass, staccato march, noir ostinato, romantic arpeggios, action ostinato) until they're physically automatic.

Give the Button

When an actor goes for a button (the closing punchline), hit a musical button with them — a sharp chord, a glissando, a sting. Practice listening for the physical "button gesture" and matching it instantly.

"The best improv music is invisible when it's working — and obvious when it's not."
